Problem Proposal:

Piazza is a learning management tool that allows students and teachers to communicate through a forum-type message board. Students often use this service to post questions, contribute to in-class discussions, and complete and/or upload assignments. They’re also able to access resources professors upload and communicate with the instructors privately. While the platform’s purpose is to create a centralized place for information and communication, the structure of the content and accessibility makes it difficult to navigate through the information. Altering the layout and sizing of the page will allow an easier experience for students to get the needed content therefore would be worth the change. The view of the website for teachers and for TAs is more effective for their use because they see a cleaner version of the messages that have been sent. The cleaner part of the TA/teacher’s view is that just the subject line is present for each message instead of the beginning of the message below the title.One potential factor that could affect the stakeholders is that the donation pop-up is not present in the cleaner teacher/TA’s view of the website while it is present in the more cluttered view of the website. Because Piazza allows for teachers to hold a platform for discussions and assignments for their students, the usability of the platform is crucial to establish effective discussions and grading for assignments. Therefore, a larger effect of this problem could be untimely grading and missed private and public discussions. Our group has identified several issues within the platform especially within the areas of readability and usability.

In terms of readability, Piazza has several issues that affect the users ability to clearly understand what is being articulated due to issues such as reading the font size, understanding the unclear language, and organizing the text. For example, when the Piazza posts are presented on the sidebar, the body of the text is small and a light gray color, so seeing what is written could be hard for users who have impaired vision. Meanwhile at the bottom of the screen, there is a toolbar to help the user gauge activity for their course. The term “special mentions” is used for informing the user that they have been mentioned, but that language is unclear and not consistent with other forum-based platforms. The content organization is also inconsistent and confusing with private messages not being grouped together for users to read on direct threads that they have started/have been included in. It is not clear what messages have been read or not read, and the advertising of their mobile application version for the platform is ill-placed, causing most users to miss that fact.

In terms of usability, Piazza has several issues that affect the experiences of those who use it (students, teachers, TAs, etc). A common theme throughout the design of the website is the icons are not matching their typical perceptions or purposes and are misleading the user. For example, the chat box icon in the toolbar leads a user to believe this is a way to create a post or privately message someone; however, it directs the user to a page dedicated to messages from companies and the user’s profile. This results in a frustrating experience for the student due to the misinterpretation of the button’s function. Another theme is that the website is misrepresenting the content and the buttons - chatting, posting, and reading. For example, the gear icon for settings exists twice on the page - both for different settings: account and class. Another usability related bug that Piazza has is that there is no discernable difference between unresolved/resolved threads. It makes it difficult to know the purpose of the types of thread, and when to use it. Most websites have their company logo reset all filters on the site and navigate you to the home page; however, Piazza takes the user to the home page but continues to filter through previous settings. This becomes a tedious task for the users to manually change the filters to their default settings. Lastly, when a feature is disabled, a student should be unaware of that feature to avoid confusion or clutter rather than seeing a button that does nothing but tell you the feature is disabled. This is shown with the statistics button in the toolbar next to classes, and takes away from the experience and adds to the clutter.

In terms of other factors that contribute to the problems aforementioned, one that contributes greatly to not being able to effectively use the website is learner self-efficacy and the students’ perception of the website. Those who use the website are left to figure out what the different features and functions are on their own, which can get confusing with the sheer amount of things that can be done on Piazza. In addition, for those who use the website and are not familiar with discussion-based forums, such as Edmodo, would find it difficult to grasp the usability of the website. Also, Piazza is a fully online platform which requires the internet. This means that those who do not have access to computers/technology and an internet connection would not be able to use it. Also, for class sizes that are particularly large with a disproportionate ratio of students to teachers, it can be difficult for the teachers to answer every single question and deal with repeated questions.
